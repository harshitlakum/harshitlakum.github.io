About Me

Computational Science • Astrophysics • Quantum • Chemistry

I am currently pursuing a Master’s in Computational Science at the University of Vienna, where I specialize in computational physics and soft matter research. My academic path is centered on the use of partial differential equations (PDEs), numerical methods, and large-scale simulations to understand complex physical systems. This work allows me to bridge rigorous mathematics with high-performance computing in order to tackle problems that span multiple domains of physics and applied science.

My current research internship focuses on the field-controlled assembly of magnetically capped Janus colloids, where I employ Brownian dynamics and hydrodynamic interaction simulations in ESPResSo to study structural transitions such as ring-to-chain formation and clustering. This project highlights my interest in statistical mechanics, condensed matter physics, and non-equilibrium systems, with the broader goal of contributing to the development of advanced smart materials.

Previously, I have worked on a variety of computational projects, including variational Monte Carlo modeling of quantum systems, interactive molecular dynamics teaching tools, and numerical solvers for PDEs implemented in Python and Fortran. These experiences have given me both theoretical depth and practical skills in simulation design, algorithm development, and data-driven analysis.

Looking ahead, my primary ambition is to pursue a PhD in computational physics/soft matter, focusing on PDE-based modeling, statistical mechanics, and simulation-driven discoveries in complex systems. At the same time, I remain open to applying my expertise in software engineering, AI/ML, and scientific computing roles in Europe, particularly in areas where numerical modeling, HPC, and data-driven methods intersect with industry applications.

With a strong foundation in Python, Fortran, MATLAB, and modern simulation frameworks, I aim to advance both fundamental understanding and applied solutions—whether in academic research or in industry—at the intersection of mathematics, computation, and physics.
